<p>Hawthorne published <cite class="al-title">The Blithdale Romance</cite> in 1852. The inspiration for the book came from his experience joining the <a href="/transcendentalism-study-guide">Transcendentalist</a> utopian community, Brook Farm in 1841, not because he agreed with the experiment, but because he needed to save money to marry his fiance, <cite class="al-person">Sophia Peabody</cite>. Interestingly, this novel was his only published in the first person, Teachers and students looking for further summary and analysis might wish to read D. H. Lawrence's chapter on <a href="/author/d-h-lawrence/book/studies-in-classic-american-literature">Hawthorne's The Blithedale Romance</a> from his book <a href="/author/d-h-lawrence/book/studies-in-classic-american-literature/summary">Studies in Classic American Literature</a>.</p><p><img alt="The Blithedale Romance, inspired by Brook Farm" title="The Blithedale Romance, inspired by Brook Farm" itemprop="image" src="https://assets.americanliterature.com/al/images/book/blithedale-romance.jpg" style="max-width:400px; float:right; margin-right: 12px; margin-bottom: 12px;" data-pagespeed-url-hash="2741604733" onload="pagespeed.CriticalImages.checkImageForCriticality(this);"> </p><p><a href="i-old-moodie">I.
