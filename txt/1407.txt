<p><cite class="al-title">My Antonia</cite> (1918) is the third book in Cather's <cite class="al-title">Prairie Trilogy</cite>, which began with <a href="/author/willa-cather/book/o-pioneers">O Pioneers!</a> (1913), and was followed by <a href="/author/willa-cather/book/the-song-of-the-lark/summary">The Song of the Lark</a> (1915). <cite class="al-title">My Antonia</cite> tells the story of several immigrant families who move to rural Nebraska. Antonia is the eldest daughter of the Shimerdas and is a bold and carefree young woman who becomes the center of narrator Jim Burden's attention. The novel offers many elements, but clearly documents the struggles of the hard-working immigrants that homesteaded the prairies, particularly the hardships suffered by women. </p><p><cite class="al-title">My Antonia</cite> provides Willa Cather with a platform for commentary about women's rights, while weaving a story where romantic interests are ultimately bandied about by the uncontrolled changes that occur in people's lives. This final book of Cather's <cite class="al-title">Prairie Trilogy</cite> is considered her greatest accomplishment.
