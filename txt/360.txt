<p><cite class="al-title">The Divine Comedy</cite> (<i>Divina Commedia</i>), often referred to simply by its first Canto, <cite class="al-title">The Inferno</cite> was written somewhere between 1308 and 1321. It is considered a masterpiece of world literature, and a preeminent work of Italian literature. Dante chose to write in Italian, rather than Latin, which expanded the audience able to appreciate his vernacular style through the ages. The work continues to be required reading in most American high schools.</p><p>The poem is composed of 14,233 lines that are divided into three canticas (Ital. pl. cantiche) -- <b>Inferno</b> (Hell), <b>Purgatorio</b> (Purgatory), and <b>Paradiso</b> (Paradise) -- each consisting of 33 cantos</p><p><a href="/author/henry-wadsworth-longfellow">Henry Wadsworth Longfellow</a> translated the edition presented here. <a href="/author/dorothy-sayers">Dorothy Sayers</a>, English crime writer, also published a translation (1949-1962). <i>Fresco above by Domenico di Michelino, 1480~</i></p><p><img alt="The Divine Comedy Canto III" title="The Divine Comedy Canto III" itemprop="image" src="https://assets.americanliterature.com/al/images/book/the-divine-comedy.jpg" style="max-width:400px;
