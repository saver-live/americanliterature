<p> Ambrose Bierce is well known for his war stories: <a href="/author/ambrose-bierce/short-story/an-occurrence-at-owl-creek-bridge">An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge</a>, <a href="/author/ambrose-bierce/short-story/the-boarded-window">The Boarded Window</a>, <a href="/author/ambrose-bierce/short-story/killed-at-resaca">Killed at Resaca</a>, <a href="/author/ambrose-bierce/short-story/chickamauga">Chickamauga</a>; and his ghost stories: <a href="/author/ambrose-bierce/short-story/present-at-a-hanging">Present at a Hanging</a>, <a href="/author/ambrose-bierce/short-story/a-wireless-message">A Wireless Message</a>, <a href="/author/ambrose-bierce/short-story/a-vine-on-a-house">A Vine on a House</a>, <a href="/author/ambrose-bierce/short-story/a-baffled-ambuscade">A Baffled Ambuscade</a>. But Bierce was also able to write in other genres as well, and his 1899 publication of <cite class="al-title">Fantastic Fables</cite> anticipated the grotesquerie style that would emerge in the 20th century, exemplified by the works of <cite class="al-author">Katherine Anne Porter</cite>. </p><p> The stories in <cite class="al-title">Fantastic Fables</cite> are featured in our <a href="/fairy-tales-guide">Favorite Fairy Tales</a>, recommended for ages 13 to adult.
