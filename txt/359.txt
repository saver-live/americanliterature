<p><cite class="al-title">Ragged Dick; or, Street Life in New York with the Boot Blacks</cite> (1868) was Alger's most popular novel, following the popular serialized version published in <cite class="al-periodical">Student and Schoolmate</cite> in 1867. His most popular "rags-to-riches" story, what became an iconic symbol of "the American Dream," is a lesson to encourage young man to apply themselves to valiant work, because it might just be rewarded by a wealthy patron, making it all worthwhile. The phrase "Horatio Alger myth" comes from this idea that sometimes hard work isn't rewarded, even though it's well-deserving. The theme would be Alger's mantra throughout his writing career, flagging in popularity due to its redundancy. Same story, different settings. The term "boot black" means shoe shiner, a popular job for scrappy young men needing work in New York City at the time Alger resided there. <i>[Book cover pictured is the 1895 edition.]</i></p><p><img alt="Horatio Alger, Ragged Dick serial" title="Horatio Alger, Ragged Dick serial" itemprop="image" src="https://assets.americanliterature.com/al/images/book/ragged-dick-serial.jpg" style="max-width:450px; float:right; margin-left: 12px; margin-bottom: 12px;" data-pagespeed-url-hash="394068128" onload="pagespeed.CriticalImages.checkImageForCriticality(this);"> </p><p><a href="preface">Preface</a></p><p><a href="chapter-i-ragged-dick-is-introduced-to-the-reader">Chapter I -
