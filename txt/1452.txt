<p><i>I.</i></p>
<p><i>When shadows pass across the grass<br> And April breezes stir the sedge,<br> Along the brimming river's edge<br>  I trail my line for silver trout,<br> And smoke, and dream of you, my lass,<br>  And wonder why we two fell out,<br>  And how the deuce it came about.</i></p>
<p><i>II.</i></p>
<p><i>When swallows sheer the meadow-mere<br> And thickets thrill with thrushes' hymns,<br> Along the mill-pond's reedy rims<br>  I trail my line for shining dace;<br> But how can finny fishes cheer<br>  A fellow, if he find no grace<br>  In your sweet eyes and your dear face?</i></p>
<p><i>III.</i></p>
<p><i>Let thrushes wing their way and sing<br> Where cresses freshen pebbled nooks;<br> By silent rills and singing brooks<br>  I pass my way alone, alas!<br> With your dear name the woodlands ring—<br>  Your name is murmured by the grass,<br>  By earth, by air, all-where I pass.</i></p>
<p><i>IV.</i></p>
<p><i>The painted bream may swim the stream—<br>  I'll cast no line to-day, pardi!<br> In vain the river-ripples gleam,<br>  In vain the thrushes' minstrelsy.<br> Vain is the wind that whispers, "Lo!<br> Thy fish are waiting—Angler, go!"</i></p>
<p><i>V.</i></p>
<p><i>Will you forgive if I forgive?<br> Life is too sad, I think, to live<br> Alone, and dream and smoke and fish;<br> I'll say "Forgive" first—if you wish?</i></p>
<p><i>VI.</i></p>
<p><i>For at that word, the Sorcery<br> Of Love shall change the earth and sky<br> To Paradise, with cherubim<br> Instead of birds on every limb.</i></p>
<p><i>VII.</i></p>
<p><i>Rivers shall sing our rhapsody;<br> The vaulted forest, tree by tree,<br> High hung with tapestry, shall glow<br> With golden pillars all a-row.</i></p>
<p><i>VIII.</i></p>
<p><i>And down the gilded forest aisle<br> Shy throngs of violets shall smile<br> And kiss your feet from tree to tree<br> While blue-bells droop in courtesy.</i></p>
<p><i>IX.</i></p>
<p><i>And if the sun incarnadine<br> The clouds—green leaves shall be your screen;</i><br> <span><span class="pagenum ws-pagenum" id="287" data-page-number="287" title="Page:The_Mystery_of_Choice_-_Chambers.djvu/299"></span></span><i>And if the clouds with jealousy<br> Should weep—we'll beg of some kind tree<br> A moment's hospitality.</i></p>
<p><i>X.</i></p>
<p><i>Good cheer is here, if you incline;<br> Moss-hidden springs shall bubble wine<br> While squirrels chuckle, rank on rank,<br> And strawberries from every bank<br> Shall blush to see how deep we drank.</i></p>
<p><i>XI.</i></p>
<p><i>Winds of the West shall cool our eyes<br> While every woodland creature tries<br> His voice a little, so that he<br> May know his notes more perfectly<br> When crickets start the symphony.</i></p>
<p><i>XII.</i></p>
<p><i>Through hazel glade and scented dell<br> Where brooklets ring a tinkling bell,<br> The forest orchestra shall swell,<br> Until the sun-soaked grasses ring<br> With crickets strumming string on string.</i></p>
<p><i>XIII.</i></p>
<p><i>Then, with your white hand daintily<br> Scarce touching mine, we'll leave our tree<br> And ramble slowly toward the West<br> Where our high castle's flaming crest,<br> Towering behind the setting sun,<br> Flings out its banners, one by one,<br> Signals of fire, that day is done.</i></p>
<p><br></p>
<span><span class="pagenum ws-pagenum" id="288" data-page-number="288" title="Page:The_Mystery_of_Choice_-_Chambers.djvu/300"></span></span>
<p><i>XIV.</i></p>
<p><i>Deep in that palace we shall find<br> How blind we are, how blind! how blind!<br> And how he'll laugh, who holds the key<br> To the great portal's mystery!<br> And how his joyous laugh will ring<br> When you and I shall bid him fling<br> The gates ajar for you and me!</i></p>
<p><i>XV.</i></p>
<p><i>Let shadows flee athwart the lea<br> When dark December strips the hedge<br> Along the icy river's edge;<br>  Yet, if you will forgive me, lass,<br> The world shall bloom like spring to me,<br>  Snow turn to dew upon the grass<br>  And fagots blossom where you pass.</i></p>
<p><i>XVI.</i></p>
<p><i>Swallows shall sheer the frozen mere,<br> Dead reeds along the mill-pond's rims<br> Shall thrill with summer-thrushes' hymns,<br>  While summer breezes blow apace,<br> If you will but forgive me, dear,<br>  And let me find a moment's grace,<br>  In your sweet eyes and your dear face.</i></p>
<div style="text-align:right; padding-top: 0em; margin-right:0;"> 
 <p><i>R. W. C.</i></p>
