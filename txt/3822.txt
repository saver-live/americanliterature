<p><cite class="al-title">Thirty Years a Slave: From Bondage to Freedom</cite> is an example of a literary form known as <em>"the slave narrative"</em> or <em>"captivity literature."</em> This remarkable autobiography recounts the life of Louis Hughes, born a slave in Virginia, who secretly learned to read and write, and was later able to tell his story. The book is subtitled: "The Institution of Slavery as Seen on the Plantation and in the Home of the Planter." While <a href="/author/harriet-beecher-stowe/bio-books-stories">Harriet Beecher Stowe</a> had captivated the nation with <a href="/author/harriet-beecher-stowe/book/uncle-toms-cabin-or-life-among-the-lowly/summary">Uncle Tom's Cabin</a> this and other slave narratives were critically important in the drive for emancipation in providing credible and graphic details of their experiences, corroborating and strengthening the argument against slavery. <br><i>[Illustration of Virginia plantation owner family visiting slave quarters, <cite class="al-periodical">Harper's Magazine</cite>, August 1876.]</i></p><p>Visit <a href="/history">American History</a> and the <a href="/african-american-library">African American Library</a> for other important historical documents and figures which helped shape America.
