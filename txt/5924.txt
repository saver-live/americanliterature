<pre>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;I

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;When I remember them, those friends of mine,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Who are no longer here, the noble three,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Who half my life were more than friends to me,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;And whose discourse was like a generous wine,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;I most of all remember the divine
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Something, that shone in them, and made us see
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The archetypal man, and what might be
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The amplitude of Nature's first design.
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;In vain I stretch my hands to clasp their hands;
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;I cannot find them.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Nothing now is left
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;But a majestic memory.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;They meanwhile
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Wander together in Elysian lands,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Perchance remembering me, who am bereft
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Of their dear presence, and, remembering, smile.


&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;II

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;In Attica thy birthplace should have been,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Or the Ionian Isles, or where the seas
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Encircle in their arms the Cyclades,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;So wholly Greek wast thou in thy serene
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;And childlike joy of life, O Philhellene!
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Around thee would have swarmed the Attic bees;
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Homer had been thy friend, or Socrates,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;And Plato welcomed thee to his demesne.
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;For thee old legends breathed historic breath;
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Thou sawest Poseidon in the purple sea,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;And in the sunset Jason's fleece of gold!
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;O, what hadst thou to do with cruel Death,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Who wast so full of life, or Death with thee,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;That thou shouldst die before thou hadst grown old!


&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;III

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;I stand again on the familiar shore,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;And hear the waves of the distracted sea
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Piteously calling and lamenting thee,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;And waiting restless at thy cottage door.
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The rocks, the sea-weed on the ocean floor,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The willows in the meadow, and the free
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Wild winds of the Atlantic welcome me;
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Then why shouldst thou be dead, and come no more?
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ah, why shouldst thou be dead, when common men
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Are busy with their trivial affairs,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Having and holding?&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Why, when thou hadst read
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Nature's mysterious manuscript, and then
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Wast ready to reveal the truth it bears,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Why art thou silent!&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Why shouldst thou be dead?


&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;IV

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;River, that stealest with such silent pace
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Around the City of the Dead, where lies
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;A friend who bore thy name, and whom these eyes
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Shall see no more in his accustomed place,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Linger and fold him in thy soft embrace
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;And say good night, for now the western skies
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Are red with sunset, and gray mists arise
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Like damps that gather on a dead man's face.
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Good night! good night! as we so oft have said
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Beneath this roof at midnight in the days
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;That are no more, and shall no more return.
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Thou hast but taken thy lamp and gone to bed;
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;I stay a little longer, as one stays
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;To cover up the embers that still burn.


&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;V

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The doors are all wide open; at the gate
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The blossomed lilacs counterfeit a blaze,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;And seem to warm the air; a dreamy haze
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Hangs o'er the Brighton meadows like a fate,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;And on their margin, with sea-tides elate,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The flooded Charles, as in the happier days,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Writes the last letter of his name, and stays
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;His restless steps, as if compelled to wait.
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;I also wait; but they will come no more,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Those friends of mine, whose presence satisfied
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The thirst and hunger of my heart.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ah me!
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;They have forgotten the pathway to my door!
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Something is gone from nature since they died,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;And summer is not summer, nor can be.

</pre>
