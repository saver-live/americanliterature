<pre>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Have you read in the Talmud of old,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;In the Legends the Rabbins have told
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Of the limitless realms of the air,--
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Have you read it,--the marvellous story
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Of Sandalphon, the Angel of Glory,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Sandalphon, the Angel of Prayer?

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;How, erect, at the outermost gates
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Of the City Celestial he waits,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;With his feet on the ladder of light,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;That, crowded with angels unnumbered,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;By Jacob was seen, as he slumbered
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Alone in the desert at night?

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Angels of Wind and of Fire
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Chant only one hymn, and expire
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;With the song's irresistible stress;
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Expire in their rapture and wonder,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;As harp-strings are broken asunder
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;By music they throb to express.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;But serene in the rapturous throng,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Unmoved by the rush of the song,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;With eyes unimpassioned and slow,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Among the dead angels, the deathless
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Sandalphon stands listening breathless
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;To sounds that ascend from below;--

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;From the spirits on earth that adore,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;From the souls that entreat and implore
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;In the fervor and passion of prayer;
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;From the hearts that are broken with losses,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;And weary with dragging the crosses
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Too heavy for mortals to bear.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;And he gathers the prayers as he stands,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;And they change into flowers in his hands,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Into garlands of purple and red;
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;And beneath the great arch of the portal,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Through the streets of the City Immortal
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Is wafted the fragrance they shed.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;It is but a legend, I know,--
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;A fable, a phantom, a show,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Of the ancient Rabbinical lore;
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Yet the old mediaeval tradition,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The beautiful, strange superstition,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;But haunts me and holds me the more.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;When I look from my window at night,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;And the welkin above is all white,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;All throbbing and panting with stars,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Among them majestic is standing
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Sandalphon the angel, expanding
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;His pinions in nebulous bars.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;And the legend, I feel, is a part
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Of the hunger and thirst of the heart,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The frenzy and fire of the brain,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;That grasps at the fruitage forbidden,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The golden pomegranates of Eden,
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;To quiet its fever and pain.
</pre>
