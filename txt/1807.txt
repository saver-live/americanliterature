<p>C. Collodi created the legendary character, <span class="al-title">Pinocchio, The Tale of a Puppet</span>. Originally titled <span class="al-title">The Story of a Marionette</span>, but most commonly simply called <span class="al-title">Pinocchio</span>, was originally published in 1880 in the first Italian magazine for children. It is about an old carpenter, Geppetto's clever invention of a puppet who has adventures as a real boy. The beloved character, the talking cricket (long before Disney's version named him Jiminy) and a fairy can help, but cannot keep him entirely out of harm's way. His assailants include a cast of assassins, a fox, a cat, and a dog. He turns into a donkey and is swallowed by a dog-fish.</p><p><img alt="Pinocchio title page" title="Pinocchio, The Tale of a Puppet, illustrated by Alice Carsey" itemprop="image" src="https://assets.americanliterature.com/al/images/book/Pinocchio-illus.jpg" style="max-width:400px; float:left; margin-right: 12px; margin-bottom: 10px;" data-pagespeed-url-hash="2497038117" onload="pagespeed.CriticalImages.checkImageForCriticality(this);"> Numerous adaptations have delighted generations of children since its original creation by Collodi, most memorable, <a class="al-title" href="http://movies.disney.com/pinocchio">Walt Disney's Pinocchio</a>. It remains one of Disney's greatest movies, released in 1940.</p><p>We've chosen the 1916 edition featuring beautiful illustrations by Alice Carsey.
